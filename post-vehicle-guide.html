Build 42 Vehicle Guide for Beginners ?How to Start, Maintain, and Not Break Your Car

Written for Build 42 2026-05-11 ~7 min read

Build 42 completely overhauled the vehicle system. The old "get in and drive" model is gone. Every car now has 18 individual parts and 4 fluid systems to manage. It sounds intimidating, but follow these steps and you'll be fine.

Finding Your First Car

Don't be picky at first. Anything that runs is a good car. Where to look:

  • Residential driveways ?Every neighborhood has a few. Mostly small sedans.
  • Gas stations ?Usually parked near the pumps, and you'll find gas cans nearby too.
  • Store parking lots ?Often have vans and larger vehicles behind the buildings.
  • Farms ?Pickup trucks. Great cargo capacity, but they burn fuel fast.

Check Before You Get In

Don't just hop in and turn the key. Right-click the car and select "Access Vehicle" to open the vehicle UI. Check these:

  • Part condition ?Green is good, yellow needs attention, red means broken and needs replacement.
  • Four fluids ?Gasoline, engine oil, brake fluid, and coolant. Top up anything below 50%.
  • Tires ?Flat tires won't get you anywhere. Use a tire pump to inflate them.
  • Battery ?Dead battery means no ignition. Find a replacement in garages or hardware stores.

The most common beginner mistake: driving without checking fluids. Running without oil destroys the engine in minutes. No coolant causes overheating. No brake fluid means you can't stop. Always check before starting.

How to Start a Vehicle

Once everything checks out:

  1. Confirm the battery works ?If it's dead, swap it out from a working car or find a new one.
  2. Check the starter motor ?If broken, the engine won't turn over through normal means.
  3. No keys? Hotwire it ?Use a screwdriver to hotwire the ignition. Higher Electrical skill makes this easier.
  4. Turn the key or complete the hotwire ?The engine should start. If it doesn't, go back and recheck parts and fluids.
  5. Listen to the engine ?Unusual sounds mean something is wrong. A healthy engine has a steady hum. Knocking, sputtering, or silence means stop immediately and diagnose.

Best Cars for Beginners

  • Small sedan ?Light, fuel-efficient, and parts are everywhere. Best starter car.
  • Van ?Large cargo space, decent speed. Great for supply runs. Slightly heavier on fuel.
  • Pickup truck ?Maximum cargo capacity, handles off-road well. Burns through gas quickly.
  • Motorcycle ?Fast, extremely fuel-efficient. Zero crash protection ?one collision and you're in bad shape.

Common Beginner Mistakes

  • Skipping fluid checks ?No oil kills an engine in minutes, not hours.
  • Ignoring warning signs ?Engine knocking, smoke from the hood, or dashboard warning lights mean pull over and check immediately.
  • Driving on flat tires ?Destroys the tire permanently and damages the suspension.
  • Overloading ?Too much weight reduces speed, braking distance, and fuel efficiency. Know your car's limits.
  • Not checking brake fluid ?Low brake fluid means brake failure. You will not stop when a zombie steps in your path.

Keep a Vehicle Kit in Your Trunk

Stock your car with these so you can handle most roadside problems without driving back to base:

  • 2 gas cans (filled)
  • Engine oil can
  • Brake fluid bottle
  • Coolant bottle
  • Tire pump
  • Basic tool kit (wrench, screwdriver, pipe wrench)

With this kit in the trunk, most breakdowns become a 5-minute fix instead of an abandoned vehicle.
